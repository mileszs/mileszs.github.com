# Music Operator

[Back to Sound Crew Hub](/northview-theater/) | [Troubleshooting](/northview-theater/troubleshooting/)

---

## What You Do

As the Sound Cues/Music Operator, you're responsible for playing all the pre-recorded sound: music tracks, sound effects, ambiance, and any backing tracks. You take cues from the stage manager and execute them with precise timing.

### Quick Reference
- Always acknowledge the standby call (nod, thumbs up, or verbal "standing by")
- Keep your hand on GO or spacebar during standby
- If you miss a cue's timing, make a quick decision: play it late, or skip it
- Never freeze up - keep the show moving
- Have a backup music source ready (phone or USB)

---

## Skills You'll Need

- Excellent timing and rhythm
- Ability to follow cues precisely
- Quick reflexes
- Organization and preparation
- Calm under pressure

---

## Before the Show

### 2-3 Hours Before Curtain

**1. Set up your playback system:**
- Computer/laptop with QLab (or whatever software you're using)
- Backup device with all files (just in case)
- Connect audio output to sound board input
- Test that sound board channel is receiving your signal

**2. Verify all audio files are loaded:**
- Open your show file in QLab
- Check that every cue loads without errors
- Test playback of first and last cue to verify everything works
- Make sure file names match your cue sheet

**3. Organize your cue sheet:**
- Print out a clean copy if it's updated
- Highlight your cues in one color
- Note any tricky timing or special instructions
- Mark places where you need to "standby"

**4. Set your levels:**
- Play through each type of cue (music, effects, etc.)
- Ask sound board operator to set input level
- Verify that your faders/volume in QLab are set correctly
- Note if any particular cues need to be louder or quieter

### 30-60 Minutes Before Curtain

**5. Run through critical cues:**
- Test any cues with tricky timing
- Verify fade times are correct
- Check any cues that overlap or transition
- Make sure GO button/spacebar is working

**6. Communication check:**
- Test headset with stage manager
- Confirm what verbal cues you'll receive
- Know the difference between "Standby Sound Q1" and "Sound Q1 GO"
- Clarify any cues that might be confusing

**7. Set your workspace:**
- Computer positioned where you can see it clearly
- Cue sheet next to computer
- Pencil for making notes
- Water bottle
- Make sure you won't accidentally bump anything

---

## During the Show

### Your Primary Responsibilities

**1. Listen for your cues:**
- Stage manager will call "Standby Sound Q[number]"
- Get your hand ready on GO button
- When you hear "Sound Q[number] GO", execute immediately
- Some cues may be visual (actor hits a mark, door closes, etc.)

**2. Execute cues precisely:**
- Hit GO at the exact moment called
- Don't anticipate - wait for the actual GO command
- For visual cues, practice the timing so you're consistent

**3. Follow along in your script:**
- Know where you are at all times
- Mark off each cue as you execute it
- Stay ahead mentally - know what's coming in the next 1-2 minutes
- If you get lost, listen to the stage manager or watch the action

**4. Monitor your playback:**
- Make sure cues are actually playing (check your meter/waveform)
- Watch for any errors or unexpected stops
- Be ready to restart a cue if it fails
- Keep volume consistent throughout the show

**5. Handle transitions smoothly:**
- Some cues fade in/out - make sure fade times are right
- If a cue needs to stop early, fade it out smoothly (don't just cut it)
- For underscoring, be ready to adjust based on actor timing

### Pro Tips

- **Always acknowledge the standby call** (nod, thumbs up, or verbal "standing by") so stage manager knows you heard it
- **Keep your hand on GO or spacebar** during standby so you don't have to search for it
- **If you miss a cue's timing, make a quick decision:** play it late, or skip it and catch the next one - don't freeze up
- **For music with vocals**, practice the first few beats in your head so you hit GO at the right tempo
- **If tech fails**, have a verbal code with stage manager (like "we're skipping that cue, moving on")

---

## After the Show

1. **Don't close anything** until stage manager calls "show complete"
2. **Make notes on your cue sheet:**
   - Any cues that were early/late?
   - Any technical issues?
   - Any changes needed for next performance?
3. **Save your work** (if you made any edits to QLab)
4. **Backup your show file** to external drive or cloud
5. **Leave system set up** for next performance
6. **Debrief** with director/stage manager about timing

## Common Challenges & Solutions

| Challenge | Solution |
|-----------|----------|
| You miss a GO cue | If it's within 1-2 seconds, execute it late. If more time has passed, let it go and catch the next cue |
| A sound file doesn't play | Quickly hit GO again. If it still fails, skip it and troubleshoot after the show |
| Actor timing is different than rehearsal | If it's a visual cue, adjust with them. If it's a stage manager call, they'll adjust |
| Sound effect is too quiet/loud | Note it for next show, but don't adjust mid-performance unless it's drastically wrong |
| You lose your place in the script | Listen to stage manager calls, watch the action on stage, find your spot, jump back in |

## For New Crew Members

- Always check the playlist **before the audience enters**
- Don't assume it's saved - click through every cue manually to be sure
- Keep your laptop **plugged in**, **notifications off**, and **sleep disabled** during the show
- Have a backup ready - even a phone with the cues and the right cable is better than silence
