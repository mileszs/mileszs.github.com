# Setting Up a Wireless Mic Channel for a Vocalist

[Back to Sound Crew Hub](/northview-theater/) | [Troubleshooting](/northview-theater/troubleshooting/)

---

In musical theater, each actor's mic needs to be set up so their voice is **clear**, **natural**, and **easy to hear**, without **feedback** or **harshness**. This guide shows how to set up a mic channel for a performer using our wireless systems: **Sennheiser EW-D** or **Audio-Technica 3100b**.

---

## Step-by-Step: Setting Up the Channel

### 1. Start with Flat Settings

- On the board, choose an **empty channel** for the actor
- Make sure the **EQ, compression, and pan are all reset to default**
- Give the channel a clear name (e.g., "Sophie", "Mic 1")

---

### 2. Set the Input Gain

- Have the actor speak or sing **in character**
- Adjust the **gain (trim)** until the level hits **green to low yellow** on the meter when they're talking or singing loudly
- Don't let it go into red (that means it's clipping)

> If the mic sounds too quiet even when gain is up, **check the gain setting on the wireless receiver** - it's okay to raise it a bit there too.

---

### 3. Apply a High-Pass Filter (HPF)

- Turn on the **HPF** (sometimes called "low cut") at around **100-120 Hz**
- This removes deep sounds we don't need - like bumps, thumps, or foot noise

---

### 4. EQ for Clarity

Start with gentle adjustments. Every voice is different, but here's a great starting point for musical theater:

| Frequency | Action | Why |
|-----------|--------|-----|
| 100 Hz | HPF (on) | Removes unwanted low noise |
| 200-300 Hz | -2 to -4 dB | Reduces "muddy" or "boxy" sound |
| 3-4 kHz | +2 to +4 dB | Adds clarity to speech |
| 6-7 kHz | -2 dB (if needed) | Tames sharp "S" sounds |
| 10-12 kHz | +1 dB (optional) | Adds "air" if it sounds dull |

**Note:**
- **Sennheiser EW-D** mics (forehead-mounted) often sound clear and bright - you may need *less* EQ
- **Audio-Technica 3100b** mics can sound slightly warmer or duller - the **high-mid boost (3-4 kHz)** becomes more important

---

### 5. Compression (Optional but Helpful)

- Compression helps keep soft and loud lines balanced
- Use **gentle settings**:
  - **Ratio**: 2:1 or 3:1
  - **Threshold**: Just below their loudest speaking/singing
  - **Attack**: 10-20ms
  - **Release**: 100-150ms

> It should *smooth things out*, not squash their voice.

---

### 6. Limiting (Only if Needed)

- A **limiter** protects the speakers from sudden loud moments
- Set the **threshold just above** the normal loud range, with a fast attack
- Not required unless you're pushing speakers hard or the actor yells a lot

---

### 7. Check for Feedback

- Walk the actor through the stage area with their mic on
- Slowly raise the fader while listening - if feedback starts, make a **narrow EQ cut** at that frequency

---

## DOs and DON'Ts for Vocal Mic Channels

| DO | DON'T |
|----|-------|
| Use HPF to clean up the low end | Don't crank gain too high - it'll distort |
| EQ gently and with purpose | Don't over-EQ just to "make it louder" |
| Adjust gain on the receiver *if needed* | Don't rely only on the fader for loudness |
| Use compression to help balance | Don't use heavy compression - it sounds fake |
| Test each mic individually on stage | Don't assume one EQ works for all actors |
| Label each mic clearly on the board | Don't let multiple channels go unnamed |

---

## For New Crew Members

- Every actor's voice is different. Some may be soft and need more gain; others are loud and need less.
- Ask a sound lead or director if something sounds off - they'll help you adjust EQ or gain.
- If you hear **feedback**: first **lower the fader**, then look at EQ.
