# Mic Wrangler

[Back to Sound Crew Hub](/northview-theater/) | [Troubleshooting](/northview-theater/troubleshooting/)

---

## What You Do

As the Mic Wrangler, you're the hands-on support person for all the wireless microphones. You help actors put mics on, swap batteries during the show, troubleshoot problems, and keep everything organized. You're part technician, part stage crew, and you need to be calm, organized, and good with people.

### Quick Reference
- Always ask permission before touching an actor
- Fresh batteries in every pack before every show
- Label everything: pack, headset, and channel must match
- If a mic dies, swap the whole pack (faster than changing batteries)
- Stay quiet backstage - actors are listening for cues

---

## Skills You'll Need

- Organization and attention to detail
- Quick problem-solving
- Good people skills (working with nervous actors)
- Ability to work quickly under pressure
- Gentle touch (mics are delicate!)
- Professional communication

## Before the Show

### 2-3 Hours Before Curtain

**For cafetorium/mobile setup:**
- Work with Sound Board Operator during initial setup
- Help unpack and organize mics at the prep table
- Verify mic pack labels match receiver channel assignments during setup
- Continue with the typical steps below

**1. Prepare all mic packs:**
- Install fresh or fully-charged batteries in every body pack
- Test each transmitter (turn on, check display/LED)
- Label each mic pack with actor name (tape or tag)
- Organize packs in the order actors will arrive to get them

**2. Set up your distribution station:**
- Table or counter backstage where actors will come to you
- Mic packs laid out in order
- Spare batteries organized and ready
- Cleaning supplies (if mics need wiping down between shows)
- Assignment sheet showing which actor gets which mic

**3. Check all cables and connections:**
- Inspect each mic cable for damage
- Make sure connectors are secure
- Check that mic capsules are clean
- Test that audio is coming through (work with sound board op)

**4. Prepare your emergency kit:**
- Spare batteries (at least 2 per mic)
- Medical tape (for securing mic elements)
- Small scissors
- Gaff tape
- Backup transmitter (if available)
- Cleaning wipes

### 30-60 Minutes Before Curtain

**5. Distribute mics to actors:**
- Call actors one at a time or in small groups
- Help them put on the body pack (see "How to Help Actors with Mics" below)
- Show them where the mute switch is (if applicable)
- Remind them: "Don't touch the mic element, don't turn it off"
- Check that mic placement is correct
- Have them do a quick test with sound board operator

**6. Final checks:**
- Verify every actor who needs a mic has one on
- Double-check battery levels on all packs
- Confirm you know where each actor is backstage
- Make sure your emergency kit is accessible
- Review which actors have quick changes or difficult costume situations

**7. Communication prep:**
- Know how to reach sound board operator quickly (radio, headset, or runner)
- Establish a signal for "battery dying" or "mic problem"
- Position yourself where you can access actors during scene changes

## During the Show

### Your Primary Responsibilities

**1. Monitor all mics constantly:**
- Listen to the show (wear an earpiece if possible)
- Watch for battery warnings on transmitters
- Be ready to respond to problems immediately
- Track which actors are on stage vs. backstage

**2. Handle battery swaps:**
- When sound board op signals "Actor X needs battery":
  - Grab fresh battery and find the actor backstage
  - During scene change or when actor is offstage
  - Quickly swap battery (have them turn pack off, replace battery, turn back on)
  - Test that it's working before they go back on
- If actor is on stage with dying battery:
  - Prepare replacement and catch them at first opportunity
  - Coordinate with stage manager if needed

**3. Manage quick changes:**
- Some actors change costumes and need mic repositioned
- Be ready with tape to reattach mic element
- Work quickly but carefully
- Double-check battery pack is still secure

**4. Troubleshoot on the fly:**
- Mic not working? Check: Is it on? Muted? Battery dead?
- Rustling sound? Mic element touching fabric
- Weak signal? Check that antenna isn't bent or covered
- If mic is truly dead, coordinate switching actor to backup system

**5. Keep actors calm:**
- If there's a problem, reassure them quietly
- Work quickly and confidently
- Give simple instructions: "Hold still, I'm adjusting your mic"
- Never blame the actor even if they did something wrong

### Pro Tips

- **Keep a battery log** during the show - note when you swap batteries so you know which ones are weak
- **Watch for sweat** - it can affect mic performance, have towels ready
- **Position yourself strategically** - be near the most likely problem areas (quick-change spot, stage entrance)
- **If an actor's mic dies completely**, coordinate with sound board op to boost another nearby actor's mic temporarily
- **Stay quiet backstage** - actors are listening for cues and need to concentrate

## After the Show

**1. Collect all mics from actors:**
- Have them return to your station
- Remove body pack and mic carefully
- Check that all equipment is accounted for

**2. Organize and store:**
- Remove batteries from body packs
- Put batteries in charger (or dispose of disposables properly)
- Coil mic cables properly
- Store body packs and mic elements in cases
- Check for any damage or issues

**3. Clean mics if needed:**
- Wipe down mic elements with appropriate cleaner
- Clean body pack cases if sweaty
- Check for makeup or tape residue

**4. Inventory check:**
- Count all mics - make sure nothing is missing
- Note any damaged equipment
- Report problems to director or tech lead

**5. Prep for next show:**
- Charge all batteries
- Replace any damaged cables or elements
- Re-label mic packs if labels came off
- Update assignment sheet for next performance

## How to Help Actors with Mics

### Body Pack Placement

1. **Ask permission first:** "I'm going to help you put on your mic pack, is that okay?"
2. **Common locations:**
   - Clipped to waistband at center back (most common)
   - In a back pocket (if costume allows)
   - Clipped to bra strap (for actresses in certain costumes - they do this themselves)
   - In a small pouch or pocket sewn into costume
3. **Make sure it's secure but not uncomfortable**
4. **Run the cable up their back, over shoulder, to their face**

### Mic Element Placement

1. **Optimal position:** 1-2 inches from corner of mouth
2. **Tape it to their face/hairline** (use medical tape or toupee tape)
3. **Cable should go from mic, around ear, down back of neck**
4. **Make sure mic element isn't covered by hair, hat, or costume**
5. **Test: Have them turn their head side to side - mic shouldn't move**

### Professional Tips

- Always work at eye level - don't tower over actors
- Explain what you're doing: "I'm going to tape this to your cheek, you'll feel a little tug"
- Be respectful of personal space
- If an actor is uncomfortable, get another crew member of their preferred gender
- Work quickly but gently - don't rush but don't dawdle
- Compliment them when they're set: "You're all set, looks great!"

## Common Challenges & Solutions

| Challenge | Solution |
|-----------|----------|
| Actor is sweating heavily and mic tape won't stick | Dry the area thoroughly, use stronger tape (toupee tape), or try different placement slightly higher on forehead |
| Actor keeps accidentally muting themselves | Tape over the mute switch or remind them repeatedly not to touch it |
| Mic element keeps rustling against costume | Adjust placement away from fabric, use small piece of foam or moleskin to buffer |
| Battery dies during a scene and you can't access actor | Coordinate with sound board op to boost other mics, prepare to swap at first opportunity |
| Actor forgets to return mic after show | Stand at stage door and collect personally, check dressing rooms, make a list of who has what |
| Actor is nervous and making your job harder | Stay calm, speak reassuringly, work confidently - your calmness helps them relax |

## For New Crew Members

- Practice placing a mic on a friend or dummy head before doing it during tech!
- Be gentle and respectful when micing someone - always ask before touching hair or costumes
- Ask the actor to speak and sing once the mic is on, and listen for anything that sounds off
- If something's broken, **don't hide it** - report it and help solve it
